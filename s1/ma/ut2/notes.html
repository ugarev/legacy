Part 1: Basic Algebra I (Chapter 3)

1.1: Understanding Algebraic Expressions

Concept: What is Algebra?
Algebra is a branch of mathematics that uses letters (like x or y) to represent unknown numbers. This allows us to study relationships and operations between quantities.

Concept: Algebraic Expressions
An algebraic expression is a combination of numbers and letters connected by operations (+, -, ×, ÷).

  • Examples: a+3, b÷5, p×q−1.

Concept: Notation and Verbs
You must be able to translate word phrases into algebraic expressions:

  • Addition (+): "Sum", "add", "plus".
    • Example: "Add a to b" is b+a.
  • Subtraction (-): "Difference", "subtract", "minus".
    • Example: "Subtract c from d" is d−c.
  • Multiplication (×): "Product", "multiply", "times".
    • Example: "Multiply g by h" is g×h or just gh.
    • Note: The number is usually written first, so m×4 is written as 4m. 1×n is just n.
  • Division (÷): "Quotient", "divide".
    • Example: "Divide j by k" is j÷k or kj.

Concept: Index Notation
This is a shorthand for repeated multiplication.

  • Definition: In an, a is the base and n is the index. It means a is multiplied by itself n times.
  • Examples:
    • "Square of m" is m2, which means m×m.
    • "Cube of n" is n3, which means n×n×n.
    • p×p×p×p×p is p5.

1.2: Simplifying Algebraic Expressions

Concept: Terms
Expressions are made of terms.

  • Terms: Parts of an expression separated by + or - signs. For 3b+5c−2d−9, the terms are 3b, 5c, −2d, and −9.
  • Constant Term: A term without any unknowns (letters), like −1 or −9.
  • Like Terms: Terms that have the exact same unknown(s) with the exact same index.
    • Examples: 6g and −g are like terms. 3h2 and −8h2 are like terms.
  • Unlike Terms: Terms that do not have the same unknown(s) or index.
    • Examples: 6g and 3h2 are unlike terms. r3 and r2 are unlike terms.

Concept: Simplifying by Combining Like Terms (Add/Subtract)
You can only add or subtract like terms.

  • **Example 1:**6g−g+3h2−8h2=(6g−g)+(3h2−8h2)=5g−5h2
  • **Example 2:**2x−4y+x+6y=(2x+x)+(−4y+6y)=3x+2y

Concept: Simplifying (Multiplication)

  1. Multiply the numbers.
  2. Multiply the unknowns, using index notation for repeated letters.
  • **Example:**5m×4×m=(5×4)×(m×m)=20m2

Concept: Simplifying (Division)
It's best to write the division as a fraction and then reduce (cancel) if possible.

  • **Example:**h÷(3k)×9k=3kh×9k=3k9hk=3h

Concept: Order of Operations
Remember to multiply/divide before you add/subtract.

  • **Example:**3p−2p×5=3p−(2p×5)=3p−10p=−7p
  • Compare with:(3p−2p)×5=(p)×5=5p

1.3: Solving Linear Equations

Concept: Solving an Equation
To "solve" means to find the value of the unknown that makes the equation true.

Method 1: Balancing the Equation
The key principle is: whatever you do to one side of the equation, you must do the exact same thing to the other side.

  • **Example (Addition/Subtraction):**x−6=7x−6+6=7+6 (Add 6 to both sides)
    x=13
  • **Example (Multiplication/Division):**2x=622x=26 (Divide both sides by 2)
    x=3

Method 2: Removing Brackets
Use multiplication to expand the brackets first, then solve.

  • **Example:**4(m−2)=m+74m−8=m+7 (Expand the bracket)
    4m−m=7+8 (Group 'm' terms on one side, numbers on the other)
    3m=15m=5

Method 3: Cancelling Denominators

  1. Find the L.C.M. (Lowest Common Multiple) of all denominators in the equation.
  2. Multiply every single term in the equation by the L.C.M. This will cancel all denominators.
  • **Example:**2y−3y=−1
    The L.C.M. of 2 and 3 is 6.
    6(2y)−6(3y)=6(−1) (Multiply every term by 6)
    3y−2y=−6y=−6

Part 2: Basic Algebra II (Chapter 4)

2.1: Understanding Formulas

Concept: What is a Formula?
A formula is an equation that shows the relationship between two or more variables.

  • Example: The formula for the area (A) of a triangle with base (b) and height (h) is A=21bh.

Concept: Subject of a Formula
The subject is the single variable that is isolated on one side (usually the left) of the formula.

  • In A=21bh, the subject is A.
  • In P=2(w+l), the subject is P.
  • In A=s2, the subject is A.

2.2: Using Formulas (Substitution)

Concept: Substitution
This is the main use of formulas. You "substitute" (plug in) the values you know for the variables, and then you solve for the one variable you don't know.

Example:
The income (P) of a promoter is given by the formula P=8000+200n, where n is the number of phones sold.

  • **(a) Find P if n=50:**P=8000+200(50) (Substitute n=50)
    P=8000+10000P=18000
    His income is $18,000.
  • **(b) Find n if P=21600:**21600=8000+200n (Substitute P=21600)
    21600−8000=200n (This is now a linear equation to solve)
    13600=200n20013600=nn=68
    He sold 68 phones.

2.3: Introduction to Sequences

Concept: Sequence
A sequence is an ordered list of numbers that follows a specific pattern.

  • Example: 5, 10, 15, 20... is a sequence.

Concept: General Term (an)
The general term, written as an, is a formula that describes the nth term of the sequence. The variable n must be a positive integer (1, 2, 3, ...).

  • For the sequence 5, 10, 15, 20..., the general term is an=5n.

Concept: Using the General Term
You can find any term in the sequence by substituting its position (n) into the general term formula.

  • Example: For the sequence an=5n+2.
    • 1st term (n=1): a1=5(1)+2=7.
    • 2nd term (n=2): a2=5(2)+2=12.
    • 10th term (n=10): a10=5(10)+2=52.
