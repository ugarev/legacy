1. Introduction to Science

Concept Description
What is Science? The pursuit and application of knowledge and understanding of the natural and social world, following a systematic methodology based on evidence.
Nature of Knowledge Scientific knowledge is based on evidence collected from experiments and observations. It can be tested and is subject to change.
Branches of Science Includes Astronomy, Geology, Chemistry, Biology, Medicine, Physics, and Meteorology.
Discovery Something that already exists but was not previously known.
Invention Something new and useful that is created from scientific discoveries.
Science & Technology Technology is the use of scientific knowledge in a practical way to solve problems or create products.

2. Scientific Investigation

Steps of Scientific Investigation

  1. Making observations and asking questions.
  2. Doing background research.
  3. Proposing a hypothesis.
  4. Designing and conducting experiments.
  5. Analysing results and drawing a conclusion.

The Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a guess about the answer to a question. A good hypothesis must be:

  • Based on information collected.
  • Testable.
  • Measurable.

Fair Experiments and Variables

To make an experiment a fair test, all variables should be kept the same, except for the one you are investigating.

Variable Type Definition Key Point
Independent Variable The factor that is intentionally changed in the experiment. There should only be ONE independent variable.
Dependent Variable The factor that is measured as the result of the experiment. It changes in response to the independent variable.
Controlled Variable All other factors that are kept unchanged or constant. Essential for ensuring the test is fair.

3. Science Laboratory & Safety

Essential Safety Equipment

Safety equipment includes the Fume Cupboard (for noxious fumes), First Aid Box, Eye Wash (for chemical spills in the eye), and different types of Fire Extinguishers (e.g., Water, Foam, Powder, CO₂).

General Laboratory Safety Rules

  • You MUST wear safety goggles at all times during experiments involving fire, chemicals, or solutions.
  • Tie back long hair and deal with loose clothing/ties.
  • DO NOT point a test tube with boiling liquid toward yourself or anyone else.
  • DO NOT smell chemicals directly.
  • DO NOT touch hot apparatus with bare hands.
  • DO NOT handle broken glass with bare hands.
  • DO NOT run or play in the laboratory.

Accident and Emergency Procedures

Incident Procedure
Cuts Clean and sterilize the wound.
Burns / Chemical spills on skin Slowly wash the affected area with running water.
Chemical spills into eye Wash the eye with water from an eye wash bottle.
Fire Alarm Tell your teacher immediately, put down all apparatus, leave, follow the fire escape route, and assemble at the designated spot.

Fire Safety

  • The Fire Triangle requires Fuel, Oxygen, and High Temperature. Removing any one of these will extinguish the fire.
  • DO NOT use water to put out an oil fire; the water can instantly boil, creating steam with oil droplets that spread the fire.

Drawing Apparatus (Cross-sectional Diagrams)

  • Draw with a sharpened HB pencil and use a ruler for straight lines.
  • Do not shade the diagrams.
  • Keep all parts in proportion.
  • Label each piece of apparatus clearly.

4. Practical Skills

Lighting a Bunsen Burner (BB)

  1. Safety Check: Ensure the rubber tubing is connected and the BB is on a heat-proof mat. Crucially, ensure the air hole is CLOSED.
  2. Light Match: Strike and hold a burning match (flame up).
  3. Light BB: Hold the match's flame above the chimney. Turn on the gas tap (keep your hand below the chimney).
  4. Luminous Flame: A yellow, irregular, and quiet flame (≈300∘C) is produced.
  5. Non-Luminous Flame: Slowly rotate the collar to open the air hole. The flame will turn blue, become quieter, and hotter (≈900∘C). Open the air hole not more than halfway until the inner blue cone is visible.

Transferring and Mixing Solutions

  • Description: Colourless refers to an absence of colour, while transparent describes how light passes through. A solution may be cloudy, clear, or form a precipitate (a solid suspension).
  • Contamination Rule: DO NOT use the same dropper to pick up different solutions. This prevents the solutions from contaminating each other.

Science involves measuring various quantities using appropriate tools and units.

6. Errors and Data Handling

Measurement Errors

Error is the difference between the true value and the measured value.

Error Type Definition How to Reduce/Avoid
Resolution The smallest value the instrument can detect (limit of reading). Use an instrument with higher resolution (smaller intervals).
Reading Error Reading falls between the markings of the instrument. Use an instrument with better/higher resolution.
Parallax Error Reading the measurement from a wrong angle/position (i.e., not at eye level). Read the measurement at eye level. For liquids in a cylinder, read at the bottom of the meniscus (the curved liquid surface).
Zero Error The tool doesn't read zero when it is not measuring anything. Check the zero reading before the experiment.
Human Reflex Error Due to the limitations of human reaction time. Use an electronic timing system.

Accuracy and Precision

  • Accuracy: How close a measured value is to the true value.
  • Precision: How close a set of measured values are to each other.

Data Processing

  • Outlier (Extreme Value): A value far away from the rest of the data. You should typically ignore the outlier when calculating the average.
  • Averaging: The final average value should be rounded to the same number of decimal places as the resolution of the measuring tool.

7. Other Scientific Methods

Observation

Scientific observation is based on our 5 senses (Sight, Hearing, Touch, Smell, Taste).

Scientific Investigation Loop

The scientific process is iterative: If the experimental results do not support the hypothesis, the scientist must modify the hypothesis and repeat the experiment.

Methods for Analysing Results

Scientists use different methods to analyse results, beyond just fair testing:

  • Fair Testing: The standard experimental method that uses Independent, Dependent, and Controlled Variables to test the effect of one factor at a time.
  • Classifying: Organizing objects or events into reasonable groups based on their similarities (e.g., classifying animals into fish, birds, mammals).
  • Pattern Seeking: Analysing data to determine whether variables show correlations (relationships).
    • Caution: Just because two variables are correlated (a pattern) does not mean one causes the other (e.g., ice cream sales and shark attacks are both correlated with hot weather, the underlying cause). Correlation does not always imply causation.
